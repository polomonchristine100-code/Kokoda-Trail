The Kokoda Track

Kokoda Track — a historic trail through the Owen Stanley Range, Papua New Guinea

Location in Papua New Guinea

The Kokoda Track (often called the Kokoda Trail) is a 96-kilometre (about 60 miles) footpath that runs through the Owen Stanley Range in Papua New Guinea (PNG). It connects the village of Owers' Corner on the northern (Papuan) side to the village of Kokoda in the south near Port Moresby.

The trail crosses dense tropical rainforest, steep ridges and narrow valleys, with altitudes ranging from near sea level to over 2,100 metres (7,000 ft). The track traverses rugged terrain and is a defining landscape of southeastern PNG.

History — Kokoda Track Campaign in World War II

In 1942, the Kokoda Track became a key battleground in the Pacific theater of World War II. Japanese forces advanced toward Port Moresby overland via the track after failing to capture it by sea. Australian soldiers — many of them militia units from Papua and Australia — engaged in a difficult defensive campaign along the trail.

  • Timeline: July–November 1942 saw a series of desperate engagements and counterattacks.
  • Conditions: Combatants faced extreme heat, monsoon rains, disease, exhaustion, and supply challenges along the narrow, muddy track.
  • Outcome: After heavy fighting, Allied forces halted and then pushed back the Japanese advance, marking a turning point in the New Guinea campaign.

The Kokoda campaign is remembered for the bravery, endurance, and sacrifice of Australian, Papuan, and other Allied troops. War cemeteries, memorials, and numerous battle sites along the track preserve that history today.

Trekking and Tourism Today

Today the Kokoda Track is a major multi-day trekking route and pilgrimage for visitors, veterans, family members, and hiking enthusiasts. Treks typically take 8–12 days depending on pace, weather, and the chosen itinerary.

Key points for trekkers:

  • Permits & Guides: Trekkers should hire licensed guides or tourism operators and obtain any local permits. Port Moresby is the usual gateway for organizing treks.
  • Difficulty: The trail is physically demanding due to long climbs, muddy, slippery sections and basic infrastructure. Proper fitness and gear are essential.
  • Cultural Experience: Treks pass through remote villages where trekkers can meet local families and learn about village life and customs.

Trekking supports local economies but responsible tourism practices are important to protect cultural and natural heritage.

Importance to Local Communities

The Kokoda Track region is home to several Papuan communities whose lives are closely tied to the land. Villages along the trail rely on subsistence farming, small-scale commerce with trekkers, and remittances from family members working in towns.

Local landowners and village leaders play a central role in managing access, guiding, and cultural presentations. Community-led initiatives and partnerships with tour operators aim to ensure that tourism benefits are shared and culturally respectful.

Environmental Protection of the Area

The Kokoda Track passes through sensitive rainforest and montane ecosystems that support high biodiversity. Environmental protection efforts focus on:

  • Conservation: Protecting native forest, water sources and habitats for endemic species.
  • Sustainable Tourism: Minimising waste, preventing erosion through trail maintenance, and limiting environmental impact from campsites.
  • Community Programs: Engaging local communities in reforestation, rubbish collection, and educating visitors.

Visitors are encouraged to follow Leave No Trace principles, support community-led conservation projects, and use reputable operators who invest in environmental protection.
